## Your Existing Methods — Applied to UFC

### 1. Raw Data Inspection
For UFC data this means checking:
- Missing values in grappling stats (takedown attempts, submissions) vs striking stats (significant strikes, accuracy %)
- Outliers — fighters with 0 takedown attempts skew grappling efficiency metrics
- Data types and consistency (is "win" encoded as binary? Is it split by decision/KO/submission?)

### 2. Descriptive Statistics
Calculate mean, median, std, min/max for both pillars separately:
- **Striking:** sig. strikes landed per minute (SLpM), striking accuracy %, striking defense %
- **Grappling:** takedown accuracy %, submission attempts per 15 min, takedown defense %

This immediately tells you which pillar has higher **variance** across fighters — high variance is a signal that it might be a stronger differentiator in wins.

### 3. Aggregation
This is very powerful here. You can aggregate by:
- **Win method** (KO/TKO vs Submission vs Decision) — do submission winners have higher grappling efficiency scores?
- **Weight class** — heavyweight fights are more striking-dominant; lightweight may differ
- **Fighter archetype** — create groups like "striker", "grappler", "all-rounder" based on efficiency thresholds

### 4. Data Visualization
The key charts for your project:

| Chart Type | Purpose |
|---|---|
| Histogram | Distribution of striking vs grappling efficiency scores |
| Box plot | Compare efficiency scores across win/loss groups |
| Scatter plot | Grappling efficiency vs Win rate — look for linear trend |
| Heatmap (correlation matrix) | See which stats cluster together |
| Bar chart | Average efficiency by win method (KO vs Sub vs Decision) |

---

## Additional EDA Methods Worth Adding

### 5. Correlation Analysis
Before your hypothesis tests, run a **Pearson correlation matrix** between all your features and the outcome variable (win rate or win/loss binary). This gives you a ranked preview of which metrics matter most — and will guide *which* hypothesis test to prioritize.

### 6. Efficiency Score Engineering (Feature Construction)
Rather than testing raw stats, build composite scores during EDA:
- **Striking Efficiency Index** = (Strikes Landed / Strikes Attempted) × SLpM
- **Grappling Efficiency Index** = (Takedowns Landed / Attempted) × TD_per_fight + Submission_attempts

Then compare these engineered features against win rate. This is standard EDA practice before modeling.

### 7. Win Rate Segmentation
Split fighters into quartiles based on their striking efficiency score, then do the same for grappling. Plot win rates across those quartiles side by side. If striking Q4 fighters win significantly more than grappling Q4 fighters, that's visual evidence before you even run a t-test.

### 8. Distribution Shape Analysis (Normality Check)
This is directly connected to your hypothesis testing phase — before choosing between a **t-test vs Mann-Whitney U test**, check if your efficiency scores are normally distributed using:
- Q-Q plots
- Skewness/kurtosis values
- Shapiro-Wilk test (small samples) or Kolmogorov-Smirnov (large)

This makes EDA and hypothesis testing feel like one continuous pipeline, not two separate phases.

---

Your reasoning is actually correct and `dropna()` is the **better choice** for your specific project. Here's why:

**Your intuition about old fights is statistically valid.** Missing values in UFC data are not random — they're systematically missing because older fights simply weren't tracked with modern metrics. This is called **MCAR vs MNAR** in statistics:

- **MCAR** (Missing Completely At Random) → imputation is safe
- **MNAR** (Missing Not At Random) → imputation introduces bias

Your case is **MNAR**. The data is missing *because* it's old, meaning imputing the mean/median would be filling in modern-era average statistics for fighters who fought in a completely different era of the sport — that's actually **more biased** than dropping them.

**Why imputation would hurt your project specifically:**

If you impute a mean takedown accuracy for a 2001 fight where no grappling stats were recorded, you're essentially inserting a fake data point into your hypothesis test comparing grappling vs striking efficiency. This directly corrupts the core question of your project.

**The one thing you should do** is just report it transparently:

```python
print(f"Rows before dropna: {len(df_fights)}")
df_clean = df_fights[['Winner', 'B_avg_TD_pct', 'B_avg_SIG_STR_pct']].dropna().copy()
print(f"Rows after dropna: {len(df_clean)}")
print(f"Dropped: {len(df_fights) - len(df_clean)} rows ({(len(df_fights) - len(df_clean))/len(df_fights)*100:.1f}%)")
```

This way in your project report you can justify the decision clearly — you're not losing random data, you're intentionally focusing on the **modern tracked era** of UFC where complete statistics exist, which actually strengthens your analysis rather than weakening it.
